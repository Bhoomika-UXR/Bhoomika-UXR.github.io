Trust, Learnability & Workflow Fit: Foundational Research for an AI-Assisted Builder Tool

Led foundational and longitudinal research to evaluate adoption readiness for a prompt-driven AI tool designed to accelerate builder workflows at a large enterprise cloud platform.

Background

Context & Problem Space

Builder teams across a large enterprise cloud platform reported significant friction with repetitive interface construction tasks. Rapid advances in generative AI prompted leadership to explore a prompt-driven tool capable of generating design-system-aligned interfaces from natural language input.

As a 0→1 initiative, the team faced high uncertainty before committing to build. Without early validation, there was a real risk of investing in technically impressive capabilities that would fail adoption.

Key uncertainties going in:

  • — Would builders trust AI-generated interfaces?
  • — How would the tool fit real, messy workflows?
  • — Did prompt-based generation match existing mental models?
  • — What level of transparency and control would users expect?
Process

Research Approach

A multi-phase study designed to capture both early expectations and real-world longitudinal behavior.

01

Concept Validation

12 participants

Semi-structured interviews with 6 designers and 6 developers to evaluate first impressions, workflow fit, mental model alignment, and trust expectations before any prototype was built.

Focus areasFirst impressions · Workflow fit · Mental models · Trust signals
OutputInsights shaped the interactive prototype for Phase 2 evaluation
02

Longitudinal Early-Access Evaluation

48 builders · 4 cohorts

Four staggered cohorts of 12 builders (designers, developers, PMs) each participated for 2 weeks. Cohorts were staggered to manage system load and capture evolving experience patterns as the product iterated live.

Week 1 — Daily Workflow Diaries

Participants documented real-world use: tasks attempted, prompts used, friction encountered, workarounds invented, and confidence levels. Diary prompts were varied to probe emerging themes and reduce fatigue.

Week 2 — Deep-Dive Interviews

12 follow-up interviews (cross-role) to explore workflow integration challenges, mental models, trust formation, and adoption barriers in depth.

03

Continuous Insight Delivery

Agile cadence

Rather than a single end-of-study readout, findings were shared continuously as they emerged. Designers, engineers, and leadership reviewed insights together during the evaluation period — enabling improvements to ship while participants were still active.

Key effect: Participants observed their feedback reflected in the evolving product. This responsiveness increased trust in the tool itself and motivated continued engagement — a finding in its own right.
Research Impact

From Insight to Decision

Each finding mapped directly to a product or roadmap decision. This is the chain from observation to outcome.

Findings

Key Insights

Three primary findings that directly shaped roadmap decisions, and three supporting findings that deepened the picture.

Primary findings — roadmap-driving

01 · Learnability

Prompt Fatigue Slowed Effective Use

Users were unsure how specific prompts needed to be, what the system inferred versus required, and how to judge output quality. Without scaffolding, users had to invent a usage strategy before they could even evaluate the tool's usefulness.

Decision influenced

Workflow-grounded onboarding & inline prompt guidance

02 · Trust

Opacity Broke Trust and Discouraged Iteration

Trust eroded when outputs appeared incorrect without explanation, when the system confirmed completion without describing its actions, and when unsupported requests weren't surfaced clearly. Opaque behavior made results feel unpredictable — discouraging the iteration that would have built fluency.

Decision influenced

Inline transparency layer; system explains its actions

03 · Integration

Workflow Fit Determined Perceived Value

Implementation-ready outputs were less useful than expected because results required adaptation to existing team frameworks and project architectures. Builders expected the tool to meet them inside their environments — not introduce additional context switching.

Decision influenced

MVP pivot to visual iteration; paused code export

Additional findings — deepened the picture

04 · Mental Models

Visual Iteration Beat Prompt-from-Scratch

Participants preferred uploading existing designs and iterating on visual starting points. Generating from a blank prompt felt slower than tools they already knew.

05 · Continuity

Session Memory Shaped Motivation

When progress was lost after closing sessions, motivation dropped and rework burden increased — directly undermining the tool's core productivity promise.

06 · Cognitive Load

Hidden Limitations Slowed Proficiency

Capabilities documented externally forced users to leave their workflow to understand what the tool supported. Trial-and-error discovery increased cognitive load across all cohorts.

Outcomes

Impact & Decisions Influenced

route

Roadmap pivot toward workflow fit

Paused emphasis on direct implementation-ready code due to integration friction. Prioritized visual starting points and rapid iteration capabilities that matched observed builder behavior.

search

Triggered deeper developer workflow research

Integration findings prompted targeted follow-up research into environment compatibility. Insights contributed to infrastructure enabling builders to access capabilities within their existing environments.

tune

Elevated usability foundations

Prioritized session continuity and improved workflow support — features that reduced rework burden and supported sustained use rather than one-off exploration.

school

Improved onboarding and inline guidance

Prompt analysis directly informed workflow-grounded examples and getting-started guidance. Inline transparency improvements reduced trial-and-error learning and context switching.

verified

Strengthened trust through rapid feedback loops

Continuous insight delivery meant improvements shipped during the evaluation period. Participants who saw their feedback reflected in the evolving product showed measurably higher trust and deeper engagement.

Researcher Reflection
"Adoption of AI-powered tools depends less on technical capability and more on mental model alignment, transparency, and workflow fit."

Builders needed to understand how the system worked, what it could and could not do, and how it integrated into real workflows — before they could trust and rely on it.

This project also reinforced the value of rapid research feedback loops. When participants saw their input reflected in improvements, trust increased and engagement deepened. Productivity tools succeed when they integrate into existing ecosystems — not when they ask users to adapt their workflows.
