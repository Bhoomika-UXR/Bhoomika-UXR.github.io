Background

Context & Problem Space

As accessibility standards strengthened across a large enterprise platform, link styling became a highly contested issue. A recent update raised concerns about legibility for users with visual and cognitive differences, potential accessibility violations, and inconsistency across teams if the standard was not widely adopted.

Some teams considered rolling back the change or introducing custom variations — creating a real risk of fragmentation across the design system. Leadership needed a data-driven decision to resolve conflicting stakeholder perspectives, ensure accessibility compliance, and maintain consistency at scale.

Without research intervention, three risks were in play:

  • — Rollback of an accessibility improvement based on perception rather than evidence
  • — Design system fragmentation as teams introduced custom link styling variations
  • — Accessibility compliance gaps across 230+ services
Process

Research Approach

A mixed-method study designed to produce evidence strong enough to resolve a contested stakeholder decision — combining statistical rigor with inclusive qualitative insight.

01

Controlled Evaluation of Styling Variations

Quantitative

Designed a controlled evaluation of multiple link styling approaches, measuring readability and recognition performance across participant groups. Evaluation criteria balanced readability, accessibility compliance, and usability to ensure findings could support a standardization decision.

Measures Readability · Recognition accuracy · Visual comfort · Scanning efficiency
Participants Inclusive of users with dyslexia and low vision alongside the broader population
02

Statistical Analysis (ANOVA)

Inferential statistics

Applied ANOVA to evaluate performance differences across styling variations and participant groups. Statistical analysis was chosen specifically because the contested nature of the decision required evidence that could withstand stakeholder scrutiny — qualitative insight alone would not have resolved the deadlock.

Why ANOVA Enabled comparison of performance across multiple styling conditions simultaneously — producing findings that were statistically defensible, not just directionally suggestive
03

Qualitative Accessibility Feedback

Inclusive methods

Collected qualitative feedback on scanning behavior, visual clarity, and comfort across participant groups — with particular attention to the lived experience of users with dyslexia and low vision. Qualitative insights contextualised the quantitative findings and ensured the standard reflected real accessibility needs, not just measurable performance.

Key contribution Qualitative feedback surfaced why spacing improvements helped — not just that they did — enabling more compelling communication of findings to stakeholders
Research Impact

From Contested Decision to Evidence-Based Standard

How three research insights broke a stakeholder deadlock and enabled an accessible standard at scale.

Findings

Key Insights

Three findings that together resolved the contested decision — each addressing a different dimension of the stakeholder debate.

Primary findings — decision-driving

01 · Inclusive Research

Increased Spacing Improves Readability for Diverse Users

Participants with dyslexia and low vision reported that minimal spacing between text and link styling made characters harder to distinguish and increased reading effort. Increasing the separation improved recognition and enabled more efficient scanning.

Decision influenced

Prevented rollback; grounded the decision in real accessibility need

02 · No Tradeoffs

Accessibility Improvements Benefit All Users Without Tradeoffs

While performance differences for users without disabilities were minimal, increased spacing improved visual comfort and readability without negatively affecting usability. Accessibility and usability were not in tension — they were aligned.

Decision influenced

Enabled confident adoption of a single standard across 230+ services

03 · Perception vs Evidence

Visual Perception Concerns Did Not Align With Usability Outcomes

Although some stakeholders perceived increased spacing as visually heavier, testing demonstrated that readability and comprehension improved without negative impact. The gap between perception and evidence was the core reason statistical rigor was needed to resolve the decision.

Decision influenced

Resolved stakeholder deadlock; research methodology adopted org-wide
